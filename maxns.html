No Surprises CD1/CD2 UK

   A new year brings a new single for Radiohead. The third single to be released off the already world-beating album is that quiet one near the end. The first song they recorded for the album, on the first day, in one take, is one of the true works of effortless genius. Of course, Radiohead are the only band who can bring together a xylophone, music-box guitar and Thom's one-and-only soaring vocals. After the mind-boggling epic that was Paranoid Android, the band don't have to worry about releasing a potentially empty sing-a-long song. They have proved themselves many many times over. Like its predecessor, Karma Police, No Surprises is a simple song with just the right balance of quality sounds. However, it is one of the most strangely cheerful songs off OK Computer - even though with vicious lines of insurrection : "bring down the government", it is a ironic, mocking song with all the feeling of tiredness and resignation - "such a pretty house... such a pretty garden with no alarms and no surprises please". A sure-fire number one Christmas song. Except it's not released at Christmas. Still - you can't have everything...

   The first B-side, Palo Alto, opens with a squeal of electronic noise reminiscent of Airbag. In the song, you can really feel the DJ Shadow-esque influences and near tendency to loop which the band have been talking about. As can be frequent with these types of song, the tune gets sorrowfully lost among the mess. Not so. Radiohead now turning themselves to yet another style of music bring their crunching guitars (throwing back to The Bends and even Pablo Honey) and of course great melody carried by Thom and backed up beautifully by Ed. The guitar work is mind-meltingly intricate, reminiscent of Maquiladora and the squeakings sound like Pearly*. The whole song is warm and optimistic, something that Radiohead are hardly ever accused of these days. As long as Radiohead keep producing quality B-sides like these, they will stay miles ahead of the rest.

   The second song couldn't be more of a contrast. How I Made My Millions is the twisted offcut of Fitter Happier. Thom sits at his off-tune piano while the rest of the band clunk around in the background, washing dishes, and he moans (an unfortunate term which just has to fit) along to a truly aching tune. It achieves the impossible, being even simpler than No Surprises, yet achieving a quite incredible result. This is bleakness, this is sadness, this is sorrow. Sure, it is a tad indulgent, along with all the 'session' tape hiss, but it truly is a wonderful song, the likes of which haven't been seen since You Never Wash Up After Yourself.

   The live versions of Airbag and Lucky have obviously been hand-picked to be the best possible, and while keeping fairly close to the album versions, they are a good taste of the punch that Radiohead packs on the stage.

   All in all, a return to excellent form from the slightly disappointing bundle with the Karma Police singles - these B-sides would not look out of place on any album, and are well worth your cash.
