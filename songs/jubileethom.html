"We were patronised, trivialised and bullied by the G8 and the media"

     I recently became involved in the Jubilee 2000 campaign and travelled to Cologne on June 18 to show my support. I was one of a chosen delegation, along with Bono and Youssou N'Dour, who submitted the petition to the G8 summit of world leaders. There were 17 million signatures on that petition and 50,000 people in a human chain around Cologne, yet we were patronised, trivialised and bullied by the G8 and the media.
     I became involved in Jubilee 2000 because it is the most formidable and constructive force yet to tackle the issue of unpayable Third World debt, the unaccountable behaviour of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, the West's stranglehold on the poorest countries of the world, and the tragic consequences of their actions on the poorest people in the world.
     On the day, escorted by armed police, we arrived at a checkpoint, were searched for weapons and asked for our passports then ushered off to meet the waiting German Chancellor, Gerhard Schroeder, stood there with the smile of a used-car salesman. We bumbled across the press forecourt and lined up for parade, all handshakes and stupefied silence.
     All hell broke loose as Bono stepped up, with zoom lenses beckoning him like a movie photocall. He and Schroeder obeyed, while Youssou and I held up a huge banner for the coalition trying to keep it visible to the journalists fixating on Bono. Then Schroeder reached his hand out to me.
     Jubilee 2000 told me that the G8 proposals were not enough but that the spin doctors were out in force demanding our congratulations. "It is a difficult game to play" they said. "We do not want to ruin the work that has already been done and offend those who claim to be on our side".
     The G8's decision is little more than creative book-keeping on debts that are already not serviced. It still remains IMF demands for the world's poorest countries to spend less on health care and education, while still betting the country's performance against any potential debt relief.
     As Schroeder held out his hand to shake mine, I had nothing to thank him or the G8 for, I thought, and I had no idea why I was there. They had given us crumbs. With 1,000 cameras and four snipers on the rooftops, I thought it wise to shake his hand.
     As Schroeder left, the press pounced on Bono who hid his alarm behind his orangeblack shades and busked his way through inane question which barely touched the subject at the heart of the day.
     "What do you think of the riots in the City of London and isn't it harmful to your campaign?" Bono handed this to me.
     "That was organized by Reclaim the Streets and this is organized by Jubilee 2000. You are a journalist and you should be able to tell the difference," I say.
     I cannot understand a media which has the nerve to condescend and dismiss as naive a growing global movement where so many have pledged their names. It seems we are not deemed to be able to comment on such higher global forces; we are merely the greater recipients of economic wisdom; those tough competitive forces we hear so much about. These are the hard facts of life in a 'global' economy. Somehow the chief economists exist on a higher moral plane and we irritating mortals should submit to it as the will of the gods. And a willing, complicit media dutifully dismiss us.
     And the day before, a willing, complicit media dismissed the Carnival Against Capitalism as a bunch of anarchists, even though much of the demo passed peacefully until the riot police arrived. And even though this was only one event in an orchestrated global day of action.
     On Saturday, with protesters in the far distance of the shot, Tony Blair told us how the news was good, we should be thankful, $70 billion is a good step forward.
     The British government went looking for good news to tell the folks back home, Blair had been embarrassed by the G8 resolutions on GM food, was still smarting from the Euro elections and was giving this debt cancellation the hard-sell. These are merely tasty crumbs flung off the G8's banquet table to appease the crowd. There were 17 million names on the worldwide petition and according to the British media, this was not news. They just the government spin and said thank you. Instead, we were overdosed with images of royal nuptials and Jubilee 2000 was mixed up with J18, and by inference, both parties dismissed.
     Perhaps if we had done what J18 did in the City of London, caught the authorities off guard, hadn't played by the script, hadn't been hijacked by the spin doctors then we would have got more coverage? But then what sort of coverage would that have been?
     What the media and the G8 are underestimating is the breadth and diversity of resistance to the unaccountable actions of the IMF and the World Bank. What is also being underestimated is the breadth of support for Jubilee 2000. They would be wise to stop condescending and start listening.
